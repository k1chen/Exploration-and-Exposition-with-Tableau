For the Exploration and Exposition with Tableau project, I chose to explore the EdConnect 2023 Household Survey Results dataset. This dataset contained a plethora of information, but something that I wanted to explore further was the effect of a child’s race on a household’s perception on digital devices. My hypothesis prior to delving deeper into the data was that there would be an effect because of inherent cultural differences between all-white families and families of color. This is based upon my experience as a person of color as well as conversations with friends who come from both sides of the categories — all white or families of color. I created a set of 7 questions under 3 topics to organize the datasets: one introductory question to describe the 2 categories of households, 2 questions under the topic of Digital Device Usage Patterns and Learning, 2 questions under the topic of Parent-School Interaction through Digital Devices and 2 questions under the topic of Household Digital Device Perception. These questions served as a guiding point in creating the visualizations necessary for this project.

Organization of Data and an Important Note

From original table, I used Tableau to move the questions from the columns label to rows and exported/saved the data to an Excel sheet. I reimported this sheet into Tableau as an updated data source. From there, I linked the updated sheet with labels: one for labels for answers and the other for labels for questions, as the labels.csv file that was given combined the answers and questions together. Using Tableau’s relationship line and filter functions, I was able to obtain the result to create my preliminary sheets for data concerning the total household numbers. For the specific BIPOC versus All-White categorizations, I used Excel pivot tables to calculate the numbers for each question/question option. Based on the data from the pivot tables, I created another Excel sheet and manually inputted the pivot data in order to achieve a data source to import into Tableau that could ultimately be used to create my final graphs comparing the BIPOC and All-White category data.

However, in the process of creating pivot tables, I noticed that my numbers did not 100% match the reference graphs that were made available on the EdConnect Household Survey dashboard, particularly the BIPOC vs All-White numbers — what I was interested in. My numbers were slightly off, so I dug into the original CSV file provided by Professor Kim and noticed that some of the IDs had an empty value for the BIPOC category. An example of this was ID 596, which had a value for every category except the BIPOC column. Due to the fact that the BIPOC categorization was based on a “True/False” system, in which the only 2 possible results could have been BIPOC or All-White, it was quite confusing as to why there were some empty values. It seems as if the EdConnect dashboard counted these empty values into the “All White” category, resulting in numbers different from mine. However, I wanted my charts to accurately reflect the dataset that was given to me, so I decided to keep my data different from the given dashboard.

Reflection

I used a combination of 4 different types of visualizations to create my story. First, I used a pie chart to represent the 2 different types of households being analyzed: an All-White household and a household with at least 1 BIPOC child. I chose a pie graph because of its simplicity as it makes very clear to a viewer the exact numbers and percentages of each category being analyzed. For the visualization concerning the household totals regarding the topics of Digital Device Usage Patterns and Household Digital Device Perception, I used a normal bar chart. The bar chart was a clear choice for these visualizations because it allows for viewers to clearly see the choices and their respective numbers for each question asked in part 1 of the survey. To display the total household figures for the topic of Parent-School Interaction through Digital Devices, I chose to use a highlight table because of its effectiveness in conveying a message of a strong trends using colors. It’s very clear at first glance that households sway significantly towards the side of using digital devices several times to access school information just purely by looking at the colors before the numbers. Lastly, I used stack bar charts for all of the comparisons between BIPOC and All-White households because it was the best way to have a viewer understand the ratios of each survey response between the two groups. By stacking the bar chart, viewers can clearly see whether or not there is a significant difference in number/percentage between BIPOC and All-White households in the selection choices in each of the survey questions. I also added annotations to further assist viewers in immediately understanding the percentages for each bar graph and what they represented.

In testing my original hypothesis, I looked at the data assuming that if race has a no effect or a minimal effect on household, the data should follow the same pattern as displayed in the pie chart; for each choice in the survey, there should be about a 64/36 percent split between BIPOC and All-White households since that’s the same ratio as the total number of BIPOC/All-White households. It became quickly clear that there seemed to be a trend that BIPOC families tended to stray away from technology, as there was a greater percentage of BIPOC families voting “Never,” “Strongly disagree,” or “Disagree” than the percentage that they made of the total household number. For example, this can be seen in the final visualization, concerning whether or not households saw that technology would be important in the future, where BIPOC households made up 80% of the “strongly disagree” category and 88% of the “somewhat disagree.” However, it is very important to note that although these numbers may show some correlation, there are definitely a lot more factors to consider in addition to race. The survey included other household characteristics such as the genders of the child(ren) in the household, whether or not the household qualified for financial aid in school purchases, and many others. All of these other factors definitely impact a household’s perception and usage of digital devices and technology, but my visualization project was purely meant to serve as a guideline to understanding how race also plays a role in these household perceptions.
